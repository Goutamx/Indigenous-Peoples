THEME 6 – DISPLACING INDIGENOUS PEOPLES

Introduction

This chapter tells some parts of the history of the original, native peoples of America and Australia.

In Theme 8 you studied how the Spanish and Portuguese conquered and colonised South America.

From the eighteenth century onwards, more and more areas in:

  • South America
  • Central America
  • North America
  • South Africa
  • Australia
  • New Zealand

were settled by people who came from Europe.

These European settlements were called colonies. Because of these colonies, many native people were pushed out of their homelands into other areas.

Later, when the European people living in these colonies became independent from their European “mother‑country”, the colonies turned into states or countries.

In the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, people from Asian countries also migrated to some of these places.

Today, in such countries, Europeans and Asians form the majority of the population, while the native inhabitants are very few in number.

You will hardly see native people in many towns. Most people have even forgotten that natives once lived in large parts of these countries, and that the names of many rivers and towns actually come from native languages. Examples:

  • Ohio, Mississippi, Seattle – in the USA
  • Saskatchewan – in Canada
  • Wollongong, Parramatta – in Australia

Till the mid‑twentieth century, school history books in America and Australia used to tell the story of how Europeans “discovered” the Americas and Australia.

These books almost never mentioned native people, except to suggest that the natives were hostile to Europeans.

However, from the 1840s, anthropologists (people who study human societies and cultures) in America began to study these native peoples.

Much later, from the 1960s, native communities were encouraged to:

  • write their own histories, or
  • speak them out so that others could record them.

This spoken history is called oral history.

Today:

  • You can read many historical books and novels written by native people.
  • Museums in these countries often have galleries of “native art” and special sections that show aboriginal ways of life.
  • In the USA, the new National Museum of the American Indian has been organised and curated by American Indians themselves.

European Imperialism

The Spanish and Portuguese empires in America (see Theme 8) stopped expanding after the seventeenth century.

After that, other European countries – France, Holland and England – started:

  • spreading their trading activities, and
  • setting up colonies in America, Africa and Asia.

Ireland was also almost like a colony of England, because most landowners in Ireland were English settlers.

From the eighteenth century, it became clear that although the main motive for creating colonies was profit, the type of control they exercised was not the same everywhere.

Examples of different kinds of control

South Asia

  • Trading companies such as the East India Company turned themselves into political powers.
  • They defeated local rulers and annexed (took over) their territories.
  • They kept much of the old, well‑developed administrative system.
  • They collected taxes from landowners.
  • Later, they built railways to make trade easier, dug mines, and started large plantations.

Africa

  • For a long time, Europeans traded only on the coast (except in South Africa).
  • They entered the interior of Africa only in the late nineteenth century.
  • After that, European countries made an agreement among themselves to divide Africa into colonies.

The word “settler” is used for:

  • the Dutch in South Africa
  • the British in Ireland, New Zealand and Australia
  • the Europeans in America

The official language in most of these colonies was English. An exception was Canada, where French is also an official language.

Names given by Europeans to Countries of the “New World”

  • “America”
    Name first used after the travel accounts of Amerigo Vespucci (1451–1512) were published.
  • “Canada”
    From kanata, meaning “village” in the language of the Huron‑Iroquois people. This word was heard and used by the explorer Jacques Cartier in 1535.
  • “Australia”
    Sixteenth‑century name for a land in the Great Southern Ocean. The word austral is Latin for “south”.
  • “New Zealand”
    Name given by Abel Tasman of Holland, the first European to see these islands in 1642. zee is Dutch for “sea”.

A reference book called the Geographical Dictionary (pages 805–22) lists over a hundred place‑names in the Americas and Australia that begin with the word “New”.

North America

The Land

The continent of North America stretches:

  • from the Arctic Circle in the north to the Tropic of Cancer in the south
  • from the Pacific Ocean in the west to the Atlantic Ocean in the east

Important physical features:

  • To the west is the Rocky Mountain chain. West of this chain lies the desert of Arizona and Nevada. Still further west are the Sierra Nevada mountains.
  • To the east are: the Great Plains, the Great Lakes, the valleys of the Mississippi and the Ohio rivers, and the Appalachian Mountains.
  • To the south lies Mexico.
  • About 40 per cent of Canada is covered with forests.
  • There are rich oil, gas and mineral resources in many areas. This explains why there are many big industries in both the USA and Canada.
  • Today, crops such as wheat, corn and fruit are widely grown, and fishing is a major industry in Canada.

However, mining, industry and large‑scale agriculture developed only in the last 200 years, and they were started mainly by immigrants from Europe, Africa and China.

Before the Europeans ever heard of North America, people had already been living there for thousands of years.

The Native Peoples

The first inhabitants of North America came from Asia over 30,000 years ago.

They crossed into America over a land‑bridge across the Bering Straits. During the last Ice Age, about 10,000 years ago, they moved further south.

The oldest object (artefact) found in America – a stone arrow‑point – is about 11,000 years old.

The population began to increase around 5,000 years ago, when the climate became more stable.

A historian’s description of America before Europeans came

Historian William Macleish, in his book The Day before America, describes life before Europeans arrived:

On the evening of the day before “America” (that is, before Europeans reached it and gave it that name), there was diversity everywhere. People spoke more than a hundred different languages. They lived in every possible mix of hunting, fishing, gathering, gardening and farming. The quality of the soil and the effort needed to clear and farm it partly decided how people lived. Their cultures and social ideas also guided their choices. Extra supplies of fish, grain, garden plants or meat helped some societies to become powerful and layered, while others remained simpler. Some cultures had already existed for thousands of years.

Everyday life

  • People usually lived in bands or villages, often along river valleys.
  • They ate fish and meat, and grew vegetables and maize (corn).
  • They went on long journeys to hunt animals, mainly bison and wild buffalo that roamed the grasslands.

Hunting bison became easier in the seventeenth century, when natives started riding horses bought from Spanish settlers. However, they killed only as many animals as they needed for food.

The word “native” means a person who is born in the place where he or she lives.

Till the early twentieth century, Europeans commonly used the word “native” to refer to the people living in the countries they had colonised.

Society, Land and Culture

  • Native people did not practise large‑scale agriculture. Because they did not produce big surpluses of food, they did not build large kingdoms or empires like those in Central and South America.
  • Sometimes there were disputes between tribes over territory, but in general, control over land was not a major issue.
  • They were satisfied with the food and shelter the land gave them and did not feel a need to “own” land as private property.

A key feature of their tradition was making formal alliances and friendships, and exchanging gifts.

They usually did not buy and sell goods as in a market; important goods were given as gifts.

Language, beliefs and skills

  • Many different languages were spoken in North America, but these were not written down.
  • People believed that time moved in cycles. Each tribe had its own stories about how it originated, and its earlier history. These stories were passed down orally from one generation to the next.
  • They were skilled craftspeople who wove beautiful textiles.
  • They could “read the land” – they understood climate, seasons and landscapes the way a literate person reads a written text.

Wampum belts

A special object used by many tribes was the wampum belt:

  • made of coloured shells sewn together
  • often exchanged between tribes after a treaty (agreement) was made

Native names used in modern life

Names of native tribes are often given to things that have nothing to do with them, for example:

  • Dakota – the name of an aeroplane
  • Cherokee – the name of a jeep
  • Pontiac – the name of a car
  • Mohawk – a style of haircut

Encounters with Europeans

Words used for native peoples of the “New World”

  • aborigine
    Means the native people of Australia. From Latin: ab = “from”, origine = “the beginning”.
  • Aboriginal
    Adjective form of aborigine. Often (incorrectly) used as a noun.
  • American Indian / Amerind / Amerindian
    Native peoples of North and South America and the Caribbean.
  • First Nations peoples
    Term used in Canada. Refers to organised native groups recognised by the Canadian government. The Indians Act of 1876 used the word “bands”; from the 1980s, the word “nations” has been used.
  • indigenous people
    People who belong naturally to a place; original inhabitants.
  • native American
    The indigenous people of the Americas. This term is now commonly used.
  • “Red Indian”
    Old term used for the brown‑complexioned peoples whose land Columbus mistakenly thought was India. This term is now considered offensive.

Example of a native tribe

The Hopi are a native tribe who now live near California.

A photograph in your book shows a woman of the Winnebago tribe of Wisconsin. In the 1860s, people of this tribe were moved to Nebraska.

A Hopi story about the coming of the Spanish

According to a story told by Lee Brown in 1986, the Hopis had a prophecy written on stone tablets. It said that their first brothers and sisters who had gone away long ago would return as turtles across the land.

They would be human beings, but they would come “like turtles”.

So, when the time mentioned in the prophecy came, the Hopis gathered in a special village to welcome the turtles. They got up early and looked towards the sunrise.

They saw Spanish conquistadores (Spanish conquerors) crossing the desert, covered in armour, looking like turtles across the land.

Thinking these were their long‑lost brothers, the Hopis went out to welcome them. They offered their hands to the Spanish men for a handshake.

Instead of a friendly greeting, one Spanish man dropped a trinket (a small decorative object) into their hands.

After this, news spread across North America that hard times were coming, and that perhaps some human beings had forgotten the sacredness of all things, so that all humans on earth would suffer for it.

Early Trade Contacts

In the seventeenth century, European traders reached the north coast of North America after a difficult two‑month sea voyage.

They were relieved to find that the native peoples were friendly and welcoming.

Unlike the Spanish in South America, who were carried away by the abundance of gold, these traders mainly wanted to trade in fish and furs.

They got willing help from the natives, who were expert hunters.

Further south, along the Mississippi river, the French observed that:

  • Natives held regular gatherings to exchange handicrafts unique to each tribe and food items which were not available in all areas.

In exchange for local products, the Europeans gave the natives:

  • blankets
  • iron vessels (often used instead of clay pots)
  • guns – which helped them in hunting together with their traditional bows and arrows
  • alcohol

Alcohol was new to the natives. Many became addicted, which made it easier for Europeans to control the terms of trade.

On the other hand, Europeans picked up from the natives an addiction to tobacco.

Mutual Perceptions

How Europeans saw themselves and the natives

In the eighteenth century, western Europeans generally said that “civilised” people were:

  • literate (could read and write)
  • followed an organised religion
  • lived in towns and cities (urban life)

Using this standard, they considered the natives of America “uncivilised”.

Some Europeans, like the French philosopher Jean‑Jacques Rousseau, actually admired these natives.

He believed such people were “noble savages” – people who lived close to nature and were untainted by the corruptions of European civilisation.

The English poet William Wordsworth had another view. Although neither he nor Rousseau had ever met a native American, Wordsworth described natives as people living:

"amid wilds/Where fancy hath small liberty to grace The affections, to exalt them or refine".

In simple words, he felt that people living close to nature had limited imagination and emotion.

Thomas Jefferson’s harsh view

Thomas Jefferson, the third President of the USA and a man who lived in the same period as Wordsworth, spoke of natives in words that today would cause public anger. He wrote:

"This unfortunate race which we have been taking so much pains to civilise... have justified extermination."

So Jefferson actually suggested that natives deserved to be wiped out.

Washington Irving’s different description

Another American writer, Washington Irving, who was much younger than Wordsworth and who had actually met native people, described them very differently:

He wrote that the Indians he had seen in real life were not at all like the Indians shown in poetry.

  • Among white men, natives seemed silent (taciturn). But this was because they did not trust whites, and did not understand their language.
  • He pointed out that white men are also silent in situations where they do not feel comfortable.

He observed that:

  • Among themselves, Indians are lively and humorous.
  • They are excellent mimics and laugh a lot at the expense of the whites, who wrongly think that natives look at them with great respect.

Irving concluded that:

White men, in his experience, often treated poor Indians as little better than animals.

Different ideas about trade

To the natives, the goods they exchanged with Europeans were mainly gifts among friends, given in a spirit of friendship.

But the Europeans, who dreamed of becoming rich, saw fish and furs as commodities – goods to be sold for profit in distant European markets.

  • The prices of these goods in Europe went up or down according to supply.
  • Natives did not understand this concept of a faraway “market”.

They were puzzled when:

  • Sometimes traders gave them many things for the same amount of furs.
  • At other times traders gave them very few things.

They were also upset by European greed.

To get more furs quickly, traders had killed hundreds of beavers. Natives felt uneasy and feared that the animals might take revenge on them for such destruction.

Many native folk tales mocked Europeans. They portrayed them as greedy and deceitful. However, because these were told as imaginary stories, Europeans did not understand the criticism till much later.

European Settlers

After the first group of traders, more Europeans came who wanted to settle permanently in America.

From the seventeenth century onwards, many of these settlers were religious refugees:

  • Protestants living in mainly Catholic countries, or
  • Catholics living in countries where Protestantism was the official religion

They were persecuted in Europe and went to America to start a new life.

As long as there was empty land (vacant in their eyes), this did not cause much trouble. But gradually, settlers moved further inland, close to native villages.

They used their iron tools to cut down forests, and lay out farms.

Different views of nature

  • Natives and Europeans saw forests very differently. Natives could see paths and signs in the forest that were invisible to Europeans.
  • Europeans imagined forests cut down and replaced by cornfields.

Jefferson’s “dream” was of a country filled with European small farmers, each owning his own land.

Natives:

  • grew crops mainly for their own use, not for sale and profit.
  • believed it was wrong to “own” land as private property.

Therefore, they could not understand Jefferson’s dream. To Jefferson, this made them “uncivilised”.

Canada and the USA Become Countries

The modern countries known as Canada and the United States of America came into being at the end of the eighteenth century.

At that time they controlled only part of the land they now cover. Over the next hundred years, both countries extended their territory to reach their present size.

  • The USA gained large areas by:
    • purchasing land – for example: from France in the south (Louisiana Purchase) and from Russia in the north (Alaska).
    • war – much of the southern USA was won from Mexico.

When these acquisitions were made, no one thought of asking the consent of the native people who already lived there.

The western “frontier” of the USA was always moving westwards, and as it moved, natives were repeatedly forced to move further back.

Changing American Landscapes

In the nineteenth century, the landscape of America changed drastically.

Europeans treated land very differently from natives.

  • Many migrants from Britain and France were younger sons who would not inherit property from their fathers. They were eager to own land in America.
  • Later, large numbers of immigrants came from Germany, Sweden, Italy and other places. Many had lost their lands to big farmers and wanted farms of their own.
  • People from Poland happily worked on the prairie grasslands because these grasslands reminded them of the steppes in their homeland, and land could be bought very cheaply.

These settlers:

  • Cleared land and started agriculture.
  • Introduced crops like rice and cotton: these crops did not grow well in Europe, so they could be sold there for profit.

To protect their huge farms from wild animals such as wolves and mountain lions, these animals were hunted until they became extinct.

Settlers felt completely secure only after the invention of barbed wire in 1873, which allowed them to fence their land cheaply and effectively.

Slavery and Regional Differences

The southern part of the USA had a hot climate. It was too hot for Europeans to work outside comfortably.

From the experience of the South American colonies, plantation owners knew that enslaved natives died in large numbers.

Therefore, plantation owners in the southern USA began to buy enslaved Africans.

  • There were protests from anti‑slavery groups.
  • A ban was finally placed on the slave trade. However, Africans who were already in the USA remained slaves, and so did their children.

The northern states of the USA had a different kind of economy:

  • They did not depend on plantations or therefore on slavery.
  • Many northerners argued for ending slavery, calling it inhuman.

From 1861 to 1865, there was a Civil War between:

  • the states that wished to keep slavery, and
  • those that supported its abolition.

The abolitionists (anti‑slavery side) won.

Slavery was legally abolished, but African Americans were able to win full civil rights only in the twentieth century. It was then that segregation in schools and public transport – separate facilities for “whites” and “non‑whites” – was finally ended.

A Political Problem in Canada

The Canadian government faced a different long‑term problem – one that seemed even more urgent than the native question.

  • In 1763, after a war with France, Britain took control of Canada.
  • But now there were many French settlers in Canada. They repeatedly demanded autonomous political status (self‑government).

Only in 1867 was this issue settled, when Canada became a Confederation of autonomous states.

The Native Peoples Lose Their Land

In the USA, as more and more settlers arrived, they expanded their settlements.

The natives were often:

  • persuaded, or
  • forced

to move away after signing treaties in which they sold their land.

  • The prices paid were usually very low.
  • Many times, the Americans (here this term means the European people of the USA) cheated the natives. They took more land than was agreed, or paid less money than promised.

Even high officials in the government saw nothing wrong in taking native lands.

The Cherokee and the Georgia episode

One clear example comes from Georgia, a state in the USA.

  • Officials argued that the Cherokee tribe was governed by state laws, but could not enjoy the rights of citizens.
  • This was especially unfair because among all native peoples, the Cherokees had tried the hardest to learn English, and made great efforts to understand and follow the American way of life.

Yet they still were not given full citizenship rights.

In 1832, the Chief Justice of the US Supreme Court, John Marshall, gave an important judgment:

  • He said the Cherokees formed “a distinct community, occupying its own territory in which the laws of Georgia had no force”.
  • He added that they had sovereignty (self‑rule) in certain matters.

The US President of that time, Andrew Jackson, was famous for fighting against economic and political privilege for the rich.

But when it came to Indians, he acted very differently.

  • He refused to accept the Chief Justice’s judgment.
  • He ordered the US army to evict the Cherokees from their land and drive them to the Great American Desert.

About 15,000 Cherokees were forced to go. On this harsh journey, more than a quarter of them died.

This tragic forced march is remembered as the “Trail of Tears”.

How settlers justified taking the land

People who occupied the land taken from tribes tried to justify their actions:

  • They claimed that natives did not deserve the land because they did not use it “fully” or “to the maximum”.
  • They called natives lazy because natives did not use their craft skills to produce goods for market, were not interested in learning English, or did not want to dress in European style (which settlers called “correct” dress).
  • They argued that such people “deserved to die out”.

As settlers cleared the prairies for farmland, they also killed wild bison in huge numbers.

A visiting Frenchman wrote:

"Primitive man will disappear with the primitive animal."

He meant that native people (whom he called “primitive man”) would vanish along with the wild animals on which they depended.

Continuing dispossession

Meanwhile, natives were constantly pushed westward.

  • They were sometimes given new lands elsewhere, promised to them “in perpetuity” (for ever).
  • But whenever minerals such as lead, gold or oil were discovered on these new lands, settlers again forced the natives to move.

Many tribes had to share land that had earlier been occupied by only one tribe, which naturally led to quarrels between them. (The textbook continues this discussion on the next page.)

Life on Reservations and the Last Rebellions

Native American groups were pushed into small, closed-off areas called “reservations”. Many times, these places were not their original homelands, and they had no old ties with this land.

They did not accept this quietly.

  • In the USA, the army crushed a series of native rebellions between 1865 and 1890.
  • In Canada, people of mixed native and European descent, called the Metis, also rose in armed revolt between 1869 and 1885.

After these defeats, open armed resistance ended.

Chief Seattle’s Reply (1854) – in Simple English

In 1854, the President of the USA asked a native leader, Chief Seattle, to sign a treaty. By this treaty, a large part of the land on which Seattle’s people lived would go to the American government.

Chief Seattle sent a famous reply. In simple words, he said:

You are asking, “How much will you sell your land for?” But for us, this question itself is strange. No one can own the sky, the air, or the shining water. If nobody owns them, how can anyone buy or sell them?

For my people, every part of the earth is sacred – every bright pine needle, every sandy beach, the mist among the dark woods, every open clearing, every humming insect. All these are holy because they live in the memories and experience of my people. Even the sap that flows in the trees carries the memories of the “red man”.

When the Great Chief in Washington sends word that he wants to buy our land, he is asking for something very big from us. He promises to keep aside some land for us where we can live comfortably. He says he will be like a father to us, and we will be like his children. We will think about his offer, but agreeing will not be easy because this land is sacred to us.

The sparkling water in the streams and rivers is not just water; for us, it is like the blood of our ancestors. If one day we do sell the land, you must remember that it is holy. You must also teach your children that the land and water are holy. Every faint reflection you see in the clear lake water carries stories and memories from the lives of my people. The soft sound of the water is like the voice of my grandfather.

Note: The Rise of Anthropology

From about the 1840s, a new subject called “anthropology” (first developed in France) was introduced in North America.

  • Its aim was to study the differences between native “primitive” communities and the supposedly “civilised” societies of Europe.
  • Some anthropologists wrongly argued that, just as there were no longer any “primitive” people left in Europe, the native peoples of America too would soon die out.

(Picture on this page: a “native lodge” photographed in 1862. Archaeologists later moved this structure from the mountains and put it in a museum in Wyoming.)

The Gold Rush and the Growth of Industries

People had long believed that there was gold somewhere in North America.

  • In the 1840s, small amounts of gold were finally discovered in California (USA).
  • This started the famous “Gold Rush”. Thousands of excited Europeans hurried to America, dreaming of getting rich quickly.

To reach the goldfields and to move people and goods across the continent:

  • Railway lines were built all the way across North America.
  • Thousands of Chinese workers were brought in to work on these railways.
  • The USA’s transcontinental railway was finished by 1870.
  • The railway across Canada was completed by 1885.

The rapid growth of the USA impressed people like Andrew Carnegie, a poor Scottish immigrant who became one of the first millionaire industrialists in the USA. He famously said that while the “old nations” of Europe moved forward “at a snail’s pace”, the American “Republic thunders on at the speed of an express train”.

Why Industries Grew in North America

In England, the Industrial Revolution began partly because small peasants were losing their land to big farmers and had to move to towns for jobs in factories.

In North America, industry grew for different reasons:

  • to make railway equipment, so that fast transport could link far‑off places, and
  • to make agricultural machines that would make large‑scale farming easier.

As a result:

  • Industrial towns and cities grew.
  • Factories increased rapidly in both the USA and Canada.

In 1860, the USA was still largely undeveloped. By 1890, it had become the leading industrial power in the world.

Changes in Agriculture and Wildlife

  • Huge stretches of land were cleared and divided into large farms.
  • By 1890, the bison (buffalo) had almost been wiped out. This destroyed the hunting way of life that many native peoples had followed for centuries.

By 1892:

  • The USA’s continental expansion was complete.
  • All the land between the Pacific Ocean and the Atlantic Ocean was divided into states.
  • There was no longer any “frontier” – the open, unsettled western land which had attracted European settlers for many decades.

Soon afterwards, the USA began taking colonies outside its own continent:

  • in Hawaii and
  • in the Philippines.

The USA had become an imperial power.

Constitutional Rights in the USA

The “democratic spirit” had been the main slogan of the American settlers during their War of Independence in the 1770s.

  • This spirit of democracy came to shape how Americans saw themselves, especially in contrast to the monarchies and aristocracies of Europe (the “Old World”).
  • Another important idea was that the constitution of the USA must protect each person’s “right to property” – and that the state should not overrule this right.

However:

  • In practice, both the democratic rights (the right to vote for representatives in Congress and for the President) and the right to property were meant only for white men.

A Canadian native writer, Daniel Paul, wrote in 2000 that:

  • Thomas Paine, a powerful supporter of democracy during both the American War of Independence and the French Revolution, had actually used Indians as models for how society could be organised.
  • Paul argued that by using Native American societies as examples, Paine helped sow the seeds of democracy that later spread among the people of Europe. (He explains this in his book We Were Not the Savages, p. 333.)

Note: Karl Marx on the American Frontier

The German philosopher Karl Marx (1818–83) called the American frontier:

“the last positive capitalist utopia” – a place where the endless land and space matched the endless desire for profit in capitalism, and where this desire could freely adjust itself to that open space.

(He wrote this in his work Grundrisse, in the section titled “Bastiat and Carey”.)

The Winds of Change in North America

For a very long time, nothing really improved for the native peoples of the USA and Canada.

Report on Indian Administration

Real change only began in the 1920s.

  • A detailed study called The Problem of Indian Administration was directed by social scientist Lewis Meriam and published in 1928.
  • It came out just a few years before the major economic depression that hit the whole USA.
  • The report painted a very dark picture of life in the reservations – especially the terrible health conditions and poor educational facilities for native peoples.

The Indian Reorganisation Act (USA)

Many white Americans began to feel sympathy for the natives because:

  • natives were being discouraged from fully practising their own cultures, and
  • at the same time, they were denied the benefits of citizenship.

This sympathy helped lead to an important law in the USA:

  • the Indian Reorganisation Act of 1934.
  • This law allowed natives living in reservations to buy land and to take loans.

Debates about “Joining the Mainstream”

In the 1950s and 1960s, the governments of the USA and Canada tried a new policy:

  • They thought of ending all special provisions for native peoples.
  • Their hope was that natives would “join the mainstream” – that is, adopt European (white) culture and live like other citizens.

But the natives did not want to give up their separate identity.

  • In 1954, they prepared a “Declaration of Indian Rights”.
  • Many native peoples agreed to take citizenship of the USA, but only on two conditions:
    1. Their reservations would not be taken away, and
    2. Their traditions and customs would not be interfered with.

A similar struggle took place in Canada.

  • In 1969, the Canadian government said that it would not recognise “aboriginal rights”.
  • Native groups organised a strong opposition movement, holding demonstrations and public debates.

The dispute continued until 1982, when:

  • the Constitution Act of Canada finally accepted the existing aboriginal and treaty rights of native peoples, though
  • many details still had to be worked out.

Situation Today

Today, although the numbers of native peoples in the USA and Canada are much smaller than they were in the eighteenth century, they have:

  • asserted their right to follow their own cultures, and
  • especially in Canada, gained recognition for their rights over their sacred lands in a way that their ancestors could not even imagine in the 1880s.

A Comparison of Three Groups under European Rule

Under European control in different parts of the world, three major groups were treated unequally:

  1. Indians under British rule (in India)
    They were taxed in unfair and arbitrary ways. They were not treated as equals. The British “explained” this by saying that Indians were not yet ready to handle a representative government.
  2. Natives in America and Australia
    They were not recognised as citizens and were considered unequal. Europeans justified this by saying these peoples were “primitive” – they supposedly had no settled agriculture, did not plan for the future, and did not build towns.
  3. African slaves in America
    They were denied personal freedom. They were considered inferior. Slavery was justified by saying that it was already part of their own social systems, and by claiming that black people were naturally lower than whites.

Australia

The Native Peoples of Australia

Like the Americas, Australia has been inhabited by humans for a very long time.

  • The people we commonly call “aborigines” (a general term for many different societies) began to arrive on the continent more than 40,000 years ago, and possibly even earlier.
  • According to scientific research, they came from New Guinea, which at that time was joined to Australia by a land bridge.

However, in their own traditions, these native peoples believe something different:

  • They say they did not “come” to Australia at some later date – they had always been there.
  • The distant past is often referred to as the “Dreamtime”. This is hard for many Europeans to understand, because in these stories, the line between past and present is blurred.

Diversity of Communities

In the late eighteenth century, when the British first arrived:

  • There were between 350 and 750 different native communities in Australia.
  • Each community had its own language.
  • Even today, about 200 of these native languages are still spoken.

There is also another large group of indigenous people:

  • the Torres Strait Islanders, who live in the north. The word “Aborigine” is not used for them, because they are believed to have migrated from elsewhere and belong to a different race.

Together, aborigines and Torres Strait Islanders made up only about 2.4% of Australia’s population in 2005.

The Settlement Pattern

Australia is sparsely populated.

  • Even today, most towns and cities lie along the coast, especially the eastern coast, where the British first arrived in 1770.
  • The central parts of the continent are mostly arid desert, which is difficult to live in.

Timeline: The Europeans Reach Australia

  • 1606 – Dutch travellers first sighted Australia.
  • 1642 – Explorer Tasman landed on an island to the south, which was later named Tasmania after him.
  • 1770 – James Cook reached Botany Bay on the east coast and named the area New South Wales.
  • 1788 – The British founded a penal colony (a settlement for convicts) and established the town of Sydney.

Map of Australia (Map 2) – what it shows

A map of Australia in the book shows:

  • The main cities: Darwin in the north; Perth in the west; Adelaide in the south; Melbourne, Sydney, and Canberra in the southeast; and Hobart in Tasmania.
  • Surrounding seas: the Indian Ocean to the west, the Pacific Ocean to the east, and the Southern Ocean to the south.

Early Encounters in Australia

The story of the meetings between:

  • the European settlers,
  • the native peoples, and
  • the land of Australia

has many similarities with the story of the Americas, though it began almost 300 years later.

  • Early reports from Captain Cook and his crew described the native people they met as friendly, and the sailors seemed pleased with these encounters.
  • Later, feelings changed sharply. When Cook was killed by a native – not in Australia but in Hawaii – the British reacted with great anger.
  • As often happened in colonial history, this single incident was used to justify later acts of violence against many other native peoples.

The Sydney Area in 1790 – What Was Happening?

Historians have tried to imagine what the native Daruk people around Sydney felt in 1790. The book gives a description which, in simple language, says:

  • The British presence suddenly disturbed Aboriginal systems of food production.
  • About a thousand hungry new mouths arrived at once, and then hundreds more. This created an unprecedented strain on local food supplies.

To the Daruk people, the behaviour of the newcomers must have looked strange and frightening:

  • Sacred places were destroyed on a large scale.
  • Trees were cut down without clear purpose – not to make canoes, not for gathering bush honey, and not for catching animals.
  • Stones were moved and piled up.
  • Clay was dug out, shaped, and baked.
  • Deep holes were dug in the ground.
  • Large, clumsy buildings were being constructed.

At first, the Daruks might have tried to understand these actions through their own cultural ideas:

  • They may have thought that the cleared area was meant to be a sacred ceremonial ground.
  • Perhaps they believed that a huge ritual gathering was going to take place there – something dangerous, from which it would be wise to stay away.

In fact, it seems that the Daruk people kept their distance from the new settlement. The description suggests that later:

  • the only way the authorities could bring them back was through official kidnapping.

(Source: P. Grimshaw, M. Lake, A. McGrath, M. Quartly, Creating a Nation)

Convict Settlers and the Loss of Land

The native peoples of Australia did not realise what lay ahead:

  • In the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, nearly 90% of them would die – from diseases brought by Europeans, from the loss of their lands and resources, and in conflicts with the settlers.

Earlier, the Portuguese had tried to settle Brazil with convicts (prisoners).

  • This experiment failed because the convicts behaved violently, leading to fierce native resistance.

The British had used the same method in their American colonies until those colonies became independent. Then they shifted this practice to Australia.

  • Most of the early European settlers in Australia were convicts from England.
  • After they completed their prison sentences, they were allowed to live as free people in Australia, on one condition: they were not allowed to return to Britain.

Having no choice but to build a life in this very different land, they felt no hesitation in forcing natives off the lands which they took over for cultivation.

Timeline: The Development of Australia

  • 1850 – The Australian colonies were granted self‑government.
  • 1851 – Immigration of Chinese coolies (indentured labourers) began, but it was stopped by law in 1855.
  • 1851–1961 – Period of various gold rushes.
  • 1901 – Formation of the Federation of Australia, bringing together six states.
  • 1911 – Canberra was established as the capital.
  • 1948–75 – About two million Europeans migrated to Australia.

Economic Development and Labour in Australia

The pattern of economic development in Australia under European rule was narrower than in America.

  • Very large sheep farms and mining stations were set up over many years, with a lot of hard labour.
  • Later, vineyards (for grapes and wine) and wheat farms were added.
  • These activities became the main foundation of Australia’s wealth.

When the Australian states were united, people decided to build a new capital city in 1911.

  • One humorous suggestion for the name was “Woolwheatgold”, to show the importance of wool, wheat, and gold.
  • In the end, it was named Canberra, from the native word “kamberra”, meaning “meeting place”.

Treatment of Workers and Immigrants

  • Some native Australians worked on the big farms in conditions so harsh that they were almost like slaves.
  • Later, Chinese immigrants were brought in as cheap labour, similar to what happened in California.

However, many white settlers felt uneasy about relying on non‑white workers.

  • This fear led the governments in both Australia and the USA to ban Chinese immigration.
  • Until 1974, there was widespread fear that large numbers of “dark” people from South Asia or Southeast Asia might move to Australia.
  • Because of this fear, there was a long‑standing government policy to keep “non‑white” people out.

The Winds of Change in Australia

In 1968, a lecture by anthropologist W.E.H. Stanner shocked many Australians. His talk was called “The Great Australian Silence”, referring to the way historians had stayed silent about the story of the aborigines.

From the 1970s, similar to what was happening in North America, attitudes began to change.

  • People wanted to understand natives not just as curiosities for anthropologists, but as communities with their own cultures.
  • These cultures had unique ways of understanding nature and climate, strong community feeling, rich traditions of storytelling, and many kinds of textile work, painting, and carving.
  • Scholars argued that these should be studied, recorded, and respected.

Historian Henry Reynolds later expressed a key question in his book Why Weren’t We Told?

  • He criticised the old habit of writing Australian history as if it began with Captain Cook’s “discovery”, ignoring thousands of years of native history.

Growing Respect for Native Cultures

Since the 1970s:

  • Universities have started departments especially to study native cultures.
  • Art galleries have created sections for native art.
  • Museums have been expanded to include dioramas and imaginatively designed rooms that explain native life and culture.
  • Many natives have begun to write their own life stories.

This has been a very positive development, and it has come at a critical moment – because if native cultures had continued to be ignored, much of them might by now have been totally forgotten.

From 1974, “multiculturalism” became an official policy in Australia.

  • This means that the cultures of native peoples and of immigrant groups from Europe and Asia are all meant to be given equal respect.

A Poem about Separation

The book includes a short poem called “Two Dreamtimes”, written for Oodgeroo Noonuccal. In simple language, the speaker says to a woman named Kathy:

  • “Kathy, my sister with a wounded heart, I can’t fully express my thanks to you.” She thanks Kathy for writing down stories from the Dreamtime – stories of both happiness and sorrow – and publishing them in a paperback book.
  • The speaker remembers that, as a child, she was not allowed to play with children like Kathy, who were dark‑skinned and lived in camps by the river.
  • Because of these colour barriers, she met Kathy only late in life.
  • Only then did she begin to understand that no one had told her the land she loved so much had been taken away from Kathy’s people.

Note on Judith Wright

Judith Wright (1915–2000) was an Australian writer and a strong supporter of aboriginal rights.

  • She wrote many powerful poems about the suffering and loss caused by keeping white people and native people separate.

Human Rights, Land Rights, and Apologies

From the 1970s, the phrase “human rights” started to be used widely in meetings of the United Nations (UNO) and other international organisations.

As these ideas spread:

  • Many Australians were shocked to realise that, unlike the USA, Canada, and New Zealand, Australia had never signed treaties with its native peoples to legalise the takeover of their land.

Instead, the Australian government had always described the land as terra nullius – a Latin term meaning “land belonging to nobody”.

The “Stolen Generations” and Official Enquiries

Australia also has a long and painful history of what happened to children of mixed native and European parentage:

  • Many such children were forcibly taken away from their native families and relatives.
  • They were brought up separately, often in institutions or with white families.

Public protest over such issues led to official enquiries and finally to two important decisions:

  1. The government recognised that native peoples have ancient and deep bonds with their land, which they consider sacred, and that these bonds must be respected.
  2. It was accepted that the wrongs of the past could not be undone, but there should be a public apology for the injustice done to children in the attempt to keep “white” and “coloured” people apart.

Important Dates in Recent Australian History

  • 1974 – The official “White Australia” policy ended, and Asian immigrants were allowed to enter the country.
  • 1992 – In the famous Mabo case, the Australian High Court declared that the idea of terra nullius was legally invalid. The court recognised that natives had land rights going back to before 1770 (the year of Cook’s arrival).
  • 1995 – A National Enquiry into the Separation of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Children from their Families was held.
  • 1999 (26 May) – The first “National Sorry Day” was observed. It was a formal apology for the children who were “lost” to their families between the 1820s and the 1970s.

Short factual or concept-based questions. Click “Show answer” to reveal model answers.

  1. Q1. What is meant by the term “frontier” in the context of United States history?

    Show answer

    In American history, the “frontier” meant the open, unsettled western land of North America that lay beyond existing states. It was the moving western edge of settlement; as settlers advanced westwards, the frontier kept shifting until, by 1892, all land between the Atlantic and Pacific had been divided into states.

  2. Q2. Who were the Metis in Canada, and what major rebellion did they lead?

    Show answer

    The Metis were people of mixed native and European descent in Canada. Between 1869 and 1885 they led the Red River Rebellion, an armed revolt protesting against Canadian expansion and the way their lands and rights were being ignored.

  3. Q3. What is a wampum belt, and how was it used among native peoples of North America?

    Show answer

    A wampum belt was a belt made of coloured shells sewn together, used by many North American tribes. It was often exchanged between tribes after a treaty or agreement, serving as a symbolic record of the alliance and promises made.

  4. Q4. Why did alcohol become a serious problem in early trade between Europeans and native Americans?

    Show answer

    Alcohol was new to native peoples, and many became addicted to it. This addiction made them more dependent on European traders and allowed the traders to control the terms of trade more easily, often to the natives’ disadvantage.

  5. Q5. Why did slavery grow mainly in the southern states of the USA and not in the northern states?

    Show answer

    The southern USA had a hot climate and depended on large plantations growing crops like cotton and rice, so planters used enslaved Africans for field labour. The northern states had a different kind of economy that did not depend on plantations, so they did not rely on slavery and many northerners called it inhuman.

  6. Q6. What was the main recommendation or effect of the Indian Reorganisation Act of 1934 in the USA?

    Show answer

    The Indian Reorganisation Act of 1934 allowed natives living in reservations to buy land and to take loans. It marked a change from earlier policies that had tried to break up native lands and discourage native cultures.

  7. Q7. Explain the meaning of the phrase “terra nullius” in Australian history.

    Show answer

    Terra nullius is a Latin term meaning “land belonging to nobody”. The Australian government used it to claim that the continent had no owners, so settlers could take land without signing treaties with indigenous peoples.

  8. Q8. What do Aboriginal Australians mean by “Dreamtime”?

    Show answer

    “Dreamtime” is how many Aboriginal traditions describe the distant past when ancestral beings shaped the land and life. In these stories the boundary between past and present is blurred, and people often say they have always been in Australia, not that they migrated from elsewhere.

  9. Q9. Why is the Mabo case (1992) considered a turning point for indigenous land rights in Australia?

    Show answer

    In the Mabo case of 1992, the Australian High Court declared that terra nullius was legally invalid. It recognised that indigenous peoples had land rights going back to before 1770, when Captain Cook arrived, and so opened the way for recognising native title.

  10. Q10. Who was W.E.H. Stanner, and what did he criticise in his lecture on “The Great Australian Silence”?

    Show answer

    W.E.H. Stanner was an anthropologist who, in 1968, gave a lecture called “The Great Australian Silence”. He criticised the way historians had kept silent about Aboriginal history, writing as if Australian history began with Captain Cook’s “discovery”.

  11. Q11. Who are meant by the “Stolen Generations” in Australia?

    Show answer

    The “Stolen Generations” refers to children of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander families, especially of mixed parentage, who were forcibly taken away from their families. They were raised in institutions or with white families, as part of attempts to separate “white” and “coloured” people.

  12. Q12. What did historian Howard Spodek say about the effect of the American Revolution on indigenous peoples?

    Show answer

    Howard Spodek wrote that the American Revolution had the opposite effect on natives compared to white settlers. For settlers it meant expansion, democracy, prosperity and liberty; for indigenous peoples it meant contraction, tyranny, poverty and confinement.

4‑Mark Questions (8)

Longer questions focusing on explanation, comparison, causes–effects and significance.

  1. Q1. Compare how Europeans and native Americans looked at land, forests and nature.

    Show answer

    Native Americans saw land and nature as sacred and as a source of food and shelter rather than a thing to be owned. They did not feel a need to “own” land as private property, hunted only as many animals as they needed, and believed that every part of the earth – forests, streams, animals – carried the memories of their ancestors.

    Europeans, on the other hand, treated land as property to be bought, sold and fenced for profit. They imagined forests being cut down and replaced by cornfields, introduced large-scale agriculture and barbed‑wire fencing, and saw animals and natural resources mainly as commodities for markets in Europe.

  2. Q2. Explain the main ways in which native peoples of the USA gradually lost their lands during the nineteenth century.

    Show answer

    As more European settlers arrived, natives were persuaded or forced to sign treaties selling their lands, usually for very low prices. Americans often cheated them by taking more land than agreed or paying less than promised, and high officials saw nothing wrong in this dispossession.

    When the USA bought or conquered new regions, nobody asked native consent, and the western frontier kept moving westwards, pushing tribes back onto reservations. Even lands promised “in perpetuity” were taken again whenever minerals like gold, lead or oil were discovered, and tribes were forced to move yet again, sometimes sharing crowded areas with other tribes.

  3. Q3. Describe the causes and consequences of the Gold Rush and the building of transcontinental railways in North America.

    Show answer

    In the 1840s, small amounts of gold were discovered in California, setting off the Gold Rush as thousands of Europeans hurried to America hoping to get rich. To reach the goldfields and move people and goods, railways were built across North America, and thousands of Chinese workers were brought in to build them.

    The USA’s transcontinental railway was completed by 1870 and Canada’s by 1885, helping towns and industries to grow rapidly. This also speeded up settlement of the interior, the clearing of land for large farms, the near‑extermination of bison, and the final closing of the American frontier by 1892.

  4. Q4. How and why did industrialisation develop differently in North America compared to England?

    Show answer

    In England, the Industrial Revolution partly grew out of the enclosure of land, which drove small peasants off the countryside and into towns to work in factories. Loss of land and lack of rural work pushed people into industrial employment.

    In North America, industry developed mainly to support expansion – to produce railway equipment that could link distant areas and to make agricultural machines for large‑scale farming. By 1890, these needs had helped turn the USA from a largely undeveloped country in 1860 into the leading industrial power in the world.

  5. Q5. How did the democratic ideas of the American Revolution contrast with the actual treatment of natives, Africans and colonised Indians?

    Show answer

    The American settlers’ slogan in the 1770s was the “democratic spirit”, and they insisted that the constitution must protect each person’s right to property. But in practice, voting rights and property protections were meant only for white men, while natives and African slaves were excluded.

    Indians under British rule in India were heavily taxed and denied equality, natives in America and Australia were treated as “primitive” and not recognised as citizens, and African slaves in America were denied personal freedom and considered naturally inferior. Historian Howard Spodek summed this up by saying that expansion, democracy, prosperity and liberty for settlers meant contraction, tyranny, poverty and confinement for natives.

  6. Q6. Outline the major impacts of European settlement on the aboriginal peoples of Australia up to the early twentieth century.

    Show answer

    After the British founded a penal colony at Sydney in 1788, convict settlers and later free settlers rapidly took over Aboriginal lands for cultivation, often forcing natives off their traditional territories. Aboriginal food systems were disrupted, sacred places destroyed, and many communities faced violence.

    In the nineteenth and twentieth centuries nearly 90% of native Australians died from European diseases, loss of land and resources, and conflicts with settlers. Some natives were used as harshly treated labourers, Chinese immigration was first encouraged then restricted, and a long‑standing policy aimed to keep “non‑white” people out of Australia.

  7. Q7. Explain how attitudes towards native cultures in Australia changed from the late 1960s onwards.

    Show answer

    In 1968 W.E.H. Stanner criticised “The Great Australian Silence”, the way historians had ignored Aboriginal history. From the 1970s, people began to see natives not just as subjects for anthropologists but as communities with rich cultures, strong community life, storytelling and artistic traditions.

    Universities created departments to study native cultures, art galleries and museums opened sections for native art and life, and many natives started writing their own life stories. In 1974 multiculturalism became official policy, aiming to give equal respect to the cultures of native peoples and immigrant groups from Europe and Asia.

  8. Q8. Discuss how ideas of human rights and later legal decisions tried to address injustices against indigenous Australians from the 1970s onwards.

    Show answer

    From the 1970s, “human rights” became an important theme in international discussions, and Australians realised that, unlike the USA, Canada and New Zealand, their country had never signed land treaties with indigenous peoples. Public protests over forced child removals and other injustices led to official enquiries.

    Key steps included ending the White Australia policy in 1974, the Mabo judgment in 1992 which rejected terra nullius and recognised historic land rights, a 1995 national enquiry into the separation of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children, and the first National Sorry Day in 1999 to publicly apologise for the “Stolen Generations”.
